1. Galaxy Cluster Modeling#

1.1. Overview#

Galaxy clusters are the largest dynamically relaxed systems in the universe containing on the order of \(10^{3}-10^{4}\) galaxies. Despite their name, these systems are dominated by gas (the intra-cluster medium, ICM) and dark matter (the host halo) which make up 9-15% and 85-90% of the constituent mass respectively. These systems are dynamically bound by gravitation and supported against collapse by the hot gas of the ICM and the virialization of stellar and dark matter populations.

Note

In Pisces, various physical assumptions / paradigms may be used to construct different galaxy clusters with different properties. We currently support the following; however, we are working to expand:

  • Geometry: Galaxy clusters are currently supported in all of our PseudoSphericalCoordinateSystem subclass and our spherical coordinate system: SphericalCoordinateSystem.

1.2. Physical Properties#

Galaxy clusters are (in the simplest sense) dominated by two forces: gravity and thermal pressure. Because the system must be stabilized against collapse, these two forces must be commensurate and, therefore, knowledge of one can usually inform the other with considerable accuracy. In this section, we will describe the relevant physics of the gasseous and gravitationally bound components.

1.2.1. The Intracluster Medium (ICM)#

Assuming the intracluster medium of galaxy clusters can be modeled as an ideal fluid, the momentum density \(\rho{\bf v}\) of the gas obeys the Euler momentum equation (here written in conservative form and ignoring magnetic fields, viscosity, etc.):

\[\frac{\partial({\rho_g{\bf v}})}{\partial{t}} + \nabla \cdot (\rho_g{\bf v}{\bf v}) = \nabla{P} + \rho_g{\bf g}\]

where \(\rho_g\) is the gas density, \({\bf v}\) is the gas velocity, \(P\) is the gas pressure, and \({\bf g}\) is the gravitational acceleration. The assumption of hydrostatic equilibrium implies that \({\bf v} = 0\) and that all time derivatives are zero, giving:

\[\nabla{P} = -\rho_g{\bf g} = \rho_g \nabla \Phi\]

This core relationship forms the underpinnings of all ClusterModel instances. Effectively, a specific geometry is chosen and a combination of thermodynamic and gravitational quantities are used to solve for all of the relevant fields of the model.

1.3. Generating Models#

Using the mathematics of hydrostatic equilibrium, it is possible to start with either a set of thermodynamic properties or a set of both thermodynamic and gravitational properties and solve for all of the relevant information in the clusters. This is the underlying principle for generating models in Pisces. The model class is the ClusterModel class which implements 2 methods for initialization:

  • from_dens_and_tden(): Utilizes the properties \(\rho_g\) and \(\rho_{\rm dyn}\) to construct the cluster.

  • from_dens_and_temp(): Utilizes the \(\rho_g\) and \(T_g\) to construct the cluster.

1.3.1. From Density and Temperature#

Computing the properties of a galaxy cluster from density and temperature represents the most observationally relevant methodology, connecting the directly observable properties of clusters to the unobservable dark-sector properties of the gravitationally bound components. In Pisces, there are 4 major steps to this pipeline, all of which are preformed automatically in the from_dens_and_temp() workflow.

  1. The Equation of State is used to convert the thermodynamic properties (\(\rho_g\) and \(T_g\)) into the pressure field \(P\).

  2. Using the pressure from the previous step, the hydrostatic equilibrium assumption is applied to compute the gravitational potential (\(\Phi\)) and gravitational field (\(\nabla \Phi\)) from the pressure field.

  3. From the gravitational field and potential, the Poisson Equation is solved to determine the relevant dynamical density field.

  4. Finally, mass accounting is used to compute \(\rho_{\rm dm}\) using our knowledge of the other relevant density components.

1.3.2. From Density and Total Density#

While construction from \(\rho_g\) and \(T_g\) is highly relevant observationally, many cosmological applications are more attuned to the use of the total density \(\rho_{\rm dyn}\) and the gas density \(\rho_{g}\). In simple geometries, this approach is also numerically more robust. Like the temperature / density pipeline, the same 4 steps are applied here; however, they appear in a different order:

  1. From the dynamical density (\(\rho_{\rm dyn}\)), the gravitational field and gravitational potential are obtained from Poisson’s Equation.

  2. Mass accounting is used to compute the dark matter density (\(\rho_{\rm dm}\)) from the other relevant density components. These profiles are then integrated to determine the total mass components.

  1. Hydrostatic equilibrium is applied to convert the gravitational field into the pressure field.

  2. The Equation of State is then solved to obtain the temperature.
